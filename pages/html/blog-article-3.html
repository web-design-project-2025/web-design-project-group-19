Midnight Movies: Why We Still Love Them

Some films were never meant to be mainstream — and that’s exactly why we love them.

By Samuel Washington Cult Classics ● 17 April 2025
“They don’t ask to be liked — they ask to be remembered.” — John Waters on the legacy of cult cinema

Before streaming algorithms dictated our watchlists and blockbuster franchises dominated multiplexes, there existed a strange and sacred tradition: the midnight movie. These weren’t just films — they were rituals. You didn’t watch them because they were critically acclaimed or beautifully shot. You watched them because they made you feel like an outsider who had finally found a cinematic home.

The midnight movie phenomenon began in the early 1970s, when theaters started screening low-budget, genre-defying films late at night to attract niche audiences. It wasn’t long before titles like El Topo, Pink Flamingos, and The Rocky Horror Picture Show became more than movies — they became movements. These films invited you to shout at the screen, dress like the characters, and claim your place in a weird, wonderful subculture.

What unites midnight movies isn’t genre or style, but spirit. They’re anarchic, experimental, and often deeply flawed — but in those flaws lies their charm. From horror oddities and surreal fantasies to underground comedies and punk manifestos, these films aren’t afraid to be messy, confusing, or offensive. In fact, that’s often the point. They challenge mainstream tastes, reject polish, and carve out space for rebellion.

Take Eraserhead (1977), David Lynch’s nightmare of industrial dread and personal anxiety. Or Repo Man (1984), Alex Cox’s genre-blending satire of Reagan-era America. These aren’t just cult films — they’re cinematic fingerprints, singular visions that couldn’t exist anywhere else. They don’t care whether you “get it.” They dare you to enter their world and figure it out for yourself.

And yet, for all their weirdness, these films have endured — not in spite of their strangeness, but because of it. Today, as digital culture flattens artistic risk and emphasizes mass appeal, the cult film stands as a reminder that cinema can still be personal, confrontational, and defiantly unique. Midnight movies don’t play by the rules, and that makes them feel alive in a way few modern releases do.

Even newer films are finding cult followings. From Mandy (2018), a heavy metal fever dream drenched in crimson neon, to Titane (2021), a genre-shattering tale of grief, gender, and chrome, today’s midnight cinema continues the tradition of pushing boundaries and inviting obsession. These aren’t passive experiences — they demand emotional, and often physical, reactions.

What’s more, cult cinema is inherently communal. Whether you're at a 2 A.M. screening with a rowdy crowd or rewatching your favorite obscure title with friends who “get it,” the midnight movie creates a shared identity. It's a badge of honor. You weren’t just there — you were part of something.

In an era where so much media is polished, packaged, and algorithmically approved, midnight movies are a beautiful kind of chaos. They remind us that film isn’t just entertainment — it’s expression. Transgression. Catharsis. And sometimes, all three at once.

So as the clock strikes twelve, don’t scroll past the weird, the forgotten, or the misunderstood. Embrace them. Celebrate them. Because some stories aren’t meant for daylight.
