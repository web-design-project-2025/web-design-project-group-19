Summer 2025: What to Watch and What to Skip

Summer 2025 is shaping up to be one of the most exciting movie seasons in years.

Review Roundup ● 9 April 2025
“It’s not just about box office anymore. Audiences want depth, style, and a reason to care.” —Ava DuVernay on the new wave of summer cinema

After a few muted summers dominated by reboots and studio safety nets, 2025 is breaking the pattern — and breaking it with style. From high-concept indies to genre-redefining blockbusters, this year’s lineup proves that cinema is very much alive, evolving, and ready to surprise us again. Whether you're a fan of mind-bending narratives, feel-good comedies, or unsettling psychological thrillers, this summer offers something worth leaving the house (or hitting the stream button) for.

Let’s start with Glass Rebellion, the dystopian thriller from Boots Riley that’s already being dubbed this generation’s "Children of Men". Set in a surveillance-ridden future where emotional expression is outlawed, the film pairs razor-sharp commentary with stunning visuals. Rising star Maya El-Amin delivers a breakout performance as a resistance leader who uses illegal theater performances as a form of rebellion. It's dense, poetic, and impossible to look away from.

On a lighter — but no less impactful — note, Love Circuit, a romantic dramedy directed by Lulu Wang, has emerged as a crowd favorite. With its futuristic backdrop of AI matchmaking and memory-sharing apps, the film explores love, grief, and second chances. Starring Daniel Kaluuya and Lana Condor, it’s smart, funny, and deeply emotional without ever slipping into cliché. Critics are calling it the “first truly modern love story of the AI age.

Of course, not everything has hit the mark. Atlantica Rising, a long-hyped underwater sci-fi epic, has been visually stunning but narratively underwhelming. With a bloated runtime and dialogue that often sinks under its own weight, it serves as a reminder that spectacle without story doesn’t make waves — it makes ripples. Still, it may satisfy fans looking for immersive worldbuilding and oceanic escapism.

Meanwhile, comedy fans can rejoice in Dadland, a hilariously absurd road trip film starring Pedro Pascal and Bowen Yang as mismatched dads on a mission to reunite with their kids after a family AI miscommunication. It’s part buddy comedy, part social satire, and completely unexpected in the best way. It may not win awards, but it’s bound to win hearts — and generate a dozen TikTok trends.

One of the most interesting surprises has come from Unspoken, a near-silent drama from Swedish filmmaker Klara Holm, which follows a father and daughter navigating grief without dialogue. It’s meditative, gorgeously shot, and a reminder of what cinema can do when it leans into silence rather than noise. It’s not for everyone, but it will absolutely stay with those who let it.

So what’s the verdict? Summer 2025 is bold, strange, and refreshingly original. The industry may still be figuring out how to balance big-screen releases with streaming strategies, but one thing is clear: the creativity is back. This season isn’t just about watching — it’s about experiencing. Feeling. Thinking.

Whether you're looking to be thrilled, moved, or simply entertained, Summer 2025 is serving up a cinematic feast — and this time, the menu is full of surprises.

By Janice Lowell
